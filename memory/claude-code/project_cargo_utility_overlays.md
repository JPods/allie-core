JPods carries cargo. The network is a circulatory system — not just people, but goods in and waste out. Future MeshMobility overlay layers should include locations that generate cargo demand:

**Logistics nodes:**
- USPS post offices and distribution centers
- UPS, FedEx, DHL facilities and drop points
- Amazon fulfillment centers and lockers

**Retail/grocery:**
- Grocery stores (last-mile food delivery)
- Shopping centers and malls
- Restaurants and food service

**Utilities as cassettes:**
- Water delivery (cassette-based, no underground pipe needed)
- Sewage removal (cassette-based, continuous streaming)
- Recycling and waste (sorted fresh at source, streamed out continuously — far higher recovery rates than weekly curbside collection of rotted, compressed, mixed waste)
- Fresh waste sorted on arrival supports recycling rates impossible with current batch collection

**Why this matters for the business case:**
- Cargo is the undervalued half of every JPods network
- UPS/FedEx/DHL are natural allies — JPods cuts Middle-Mile truck cost, eliminates dead-head miles
- Water/sewage cassettes mean JPods can serve areas without underground infrastructure
- Recycling revenue from fresh-sorted waste is a new revenue stream cities don't have today
- These are measurable business lines that strengthen the CityTool ROI calculation

**How to apply:** Do NOT build these overlays yet. When ready, add as data layers in MeshMobility alongside crash/traffic/census. Each logistics node and utility point becomes a demand signal for station placement — Noelle uses them the same way she uses crash density and population.
