# 60% Mode Shift — Stress Test and Objection Register
**Established:** 2026-07-05
**Purpose:** Anticipate and prepare for challenges to the 60% mode shift claim
**Action:** Review before any investor, city council, or policy presentation

---

## The Claim

60% of current car trips shift away from cars:
- 30% captured by JPods (Middle-Mile: 1-10 mile trips)
- 30% captured by walking/biking (Last-Mile: <3 mile trips)
- US moves from 92% car → ~32% car (European levels)

---

## Combined Objection List — Weighted by Importance

### TIER 1 — Must have ironclad answers (will be asked first)

| # | Objection | Source | Why it's hard |
|---|-----------|--------|--------------|
| 1 | **"60% is a steady-state European number. How long does the ramp take?"** Year 1 won't be 60%. What's the adoption curve? If payback assumes 60% from day one, the model is dishonest. | Alice, Claude | The 2.1-year payback assumes full ridership immediately. Real ramp could be 5-10 years to reach 60%. Need a year-by-year adoption curve with payback recalculated at each stage. |
| 2 | **"Americans CHOOSE cars. This isn't forced — it's preference."** The counter-argument to "highway policy forces car use." People like their cars — privacy, flexibility, cargo space, status. | Allie, Claude | Strongest emotional objection. Need data separating genuine preference from lack-of-alternative. European cities with good transit STILL have 43-50% car use — proving ~40% of people genuinely prefer cars even when alternatives exist. |
| 3 | **"What about low-density suburbs? 0.5 mi spacing doesn't work at 400 people/sq mi."** Tulsa and most US cities are sprawling. The mesh requires density. | Allie, Noelle | JPods may only work in the urban core + dense suburban ring. Need clear density threshold below which JPods doesn't pencil out. Be honest about the coverage boundary. |
| 4 | **"Autonomous vehicles will do this cheaper without fixed infrastructure."** Waymo, Tesla FSD, Cruise — why build guideways when self-driving cars use existing roads? | Allie, Claude | This is the strongest technology competitor. Counter: AVs still create traffic, still need parking, still run on fossil fuels, still kill pedestrians (just fewer). JPods eliminates ALL of these. But the AV pitch is simpler to understand. |
| 5 | **"$554M is a lot of money. Who finances this?"** Municipal bonds? Private equity? Federal grants? The financing mechanism matters as much as the payback math. | Alice, Claude | Need a specific financing plan, not just "it pays back in 2.1 years." Revenue bonds backed by fare revenue? P3 partnerships? Tax increment financing from land value uplift? |

### TIER 2 — Serious challenges (will be asked by informed critics)

| # | Objection | Source | Why it's hard |
|---|-----------|--------|--------------|
| 6 | **"Your $1.25/km pricing is between Uber and car cost. But car cost is sunk — people already own the car."** Marginal cost of driving an already-owned car is just gas (~$0.15/mi). JPods competes against marginal cost, not total cost of ownership. | Alice | True for existing car owners. Counter: JPods + no car ownership saves $8,000-12,000/year. But the transition cost (selling car, changing habits) is a barrier. |
| 7 | **"52% of trips are under 3 miles. Those people will walk/bike, not pay for JPods."** If walking/biking captures the short trips, JPods is left with the 12-15% of trips in the 3-10 mile range. That's not 30% of all trips. | Claude | The 30% JPods capture assumes JPods gets the 1-3 mile trips too. But if walking/biking infrastructure is good enough, why pay $2-6 for a 1-2 mile JPods trip? Need clear value proposition for the 1-3 mile range (speed, weather, cargo, disability). |
| 8 | **"What about trip chaining? People don't make single-purpose trips."** Drop kids at school → drive to work → stop at grocery → pick up kids. JPods serves point-to-point, not chains. | Claude | Real objection. JPods + walking/biking handles some chains (ride to station, walk to school nearby, ride to work). But complex chains are genuinely hard without a car. |
| 9 | **"The AADT numbers count THROUGH traffic, not local trips."** A 47,600 AADT road includes trucks and interstate travelers who will never use JPods. | Claude, Noelle | Need to decompose AADT into local vs through traffic. State DOT data sometimes includes origin-destination studies. Greenville's I-85 traffic is largely through — can't count those trips. |
| 10 | **"Weather. Tulsa has ice storms, 100°F summers, and tornadoes."** Walking/biking drops to near zero in extreme weather. Does JPods surge to fill the gap or does the whole system degrade? | Allie | JPods is weather-independent (enclosed guideway). This is actually a STRENGTH — but the walking/biking 30% is weather-dependent. Honest answer: the 30% walking/biking is a fair-weather number. Winter mode: 45% JPods, 15% walking/biking. |
| 11 | **"Maintenance costs over 18 years are speculative."** The model uses 10% of capital for maintenance. Is that validated? Elevated steel structures in weather require painting, inspection, structural repair. | Alice | No operating JPods network exists to validate maintenance costs. Counter: theme park ride maintenance is well-documented (JPods regulatory model). Need specific maintenance cost data from comparable elevated systems. |

### TIER 3 — Important but addressable

| # | Objection | Source | Why it's hard |
|---|-----------|--------|--------------|
| 12 | **"ADA compliance. How do wheelchair users board a 2.3m pod?"** | Allie | Already addressed: 3 lifts per station, 40s dispatch. But need detail on pod interior accessibility. |
| 13 | **"Construction disruption. 75 miles of elevated guideway = years of street closures."** | Claude | Prefabricated spans reduce street time. Column installation is one night per column. But 2,400+ columns is still significant. |
| 14 | **"What about emergency evacuation from elevated pods?"** | Claude | Standard in theme park ride design. Need specific protocol documented. |
| 15 | **"NIMBYism. 'Not in my backyard' for elevated structures."** | Allie | Visual impact is real. Counter: guideway is smaller than power lines, provides lighting/cameras, space underneath is usable. |
| 16 | **"Cargo pods sharing guideways with passenger pods — safety conflict?"** | Claude | Weight differences (500kg max), scheduling, priority. Need clear protocol. |
| 17 | **"Revenue cannibalization — stations too close compete for the same riders."** | Alice | At 0.5 mi spacing, adjacent stations serve overlapping catchments. Net effect may be positive (convenience) but per-station revenue could be lower than modeled. |
| 18 | **"What if gas prices drop? Electric cars get cheaper?"** | Alice | JPods value proposition weakens if driving gets cheaper. Counter: congestion and parking costs don't drop with gas prices. Land use argument holds regardless of fuel cost. |

## Data We Need to Gather

| Data | Why | Source |
|------|-----|--------|
| **Year-by-year adoption curves** from new transit systems (BRT, light rail openings) | Validate ramp-up timeline | FTA National Transit Database |
| **Mode shift data from European cities** that added transit (before/after) | Prove 60% is achievable | EU Mobility Observatory, Eurostat |
| **AADT decomposition** — local vs through traffic for Greenville/Tulsa corridors | Don't count uncapturable through traffic | State DOT origin-destination studies |
| **Willingness-to-pay studies** for urban transit at $1-2/mile | Validate pricing assumption | Transportation research journals |
| **Autonomous vehicle deployment timelines** and actual cost/mile | Know the competitor | Waymo/Cruise operating cost reports |
| **Maintenance costs for elevated steel structures** (monorail, people movers, theme parks) | Validate 10% maintenance assumption | ASCE, theme park industry data |
| **Trip chaining frequency** — % of trips that are single-purpose vs multi-stop | Understand how many trips JPods can actually serve | NHTS (National Household Travel Survey) |
| **Weather impact on walking/biking** — seasonal mode share variation in European cities | Honest accounting for weather-dependent 30% | European cycling federation data |
| **Land value uplift** near transit stations (quantified) | Strengthen the fiscal argument to cities | FTA reports on transit-oriented development |
| **Financing models** for comparable infrastructure (BRT, light rail, toll roads) | Answer "who pays for this?" | Municipal bond market data, P3 case studies |

## What Else Should We Add?

Questions for the team:
1. Should we model a **phased build** (start with highest-AADT corridor, prove it, expand) instead of presenting the full $554M network?
2. Should we present the **60% as a 10-year target** with interim milestones (20% year 2, 35% year 5, 60% year 10)?
3. Should we prepare a **"conservative case"** presentation alongside the 60% case — show what happens at 15%, 30%, 45%?
4. Do we need a **specific Tulsa demographic analysis** — where are the transit-dependent populations who would adopt fastest?
5. Should we quantify the **health benefits** (reduced accidents, reduced pollution, increased walking) as economic value?
6. Should we model the **land value uplift** as a separate revenue stream (tax increment financing)?
