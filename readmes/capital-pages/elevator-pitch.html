Preparation

This is not as radical as it seems. In 1975, Congressional study PB-244854, Automated Guideway Transit, was published to identify solutions to the 1973 Oil Embargo. It identified podcar networks like JPods as the solution. It also noted that government “institutional failures” had blocked such innovations for “four to six decades.”

JPods has been breaking down those century-old barriers — with laws passed in Massachusetts and New Jersey, a vote to deploy by the DFW Regional Transportation Council, and an MOU to start building at the Mall of America.

In 1972, President Nixon sent his daughter to open the Morgantown Personal Rapid Transit network in West Virginia. Tricia Nixon's comment: "More fun than Disneyland." You can ride the Morgantown PRT today — over 100 million injury-free passenger-miles since 1975.

JPods builds robots for SkyRide, which you can ride today in Waconia, MN. SkyRide is a bicycle version of JPods created by Scott Olson — who also created Roller Blades. SkyRide is on 3 Carnival Cruise Ships and was awarded Best Cruise Ship Feature of 2016 and 2017. See photos →

In 1999, Steve Jobs wanted to show that Macs could run businesses, not just art studios. Bill's WebClerk enterprise software had won "Best New High Tech Product of the Year" from the business press in Minnesota. Bill was invited on two national tours by Apple. He tried to get Jobs to help build JPods. Jobs loved the idea of the Physical Internet® but thought Segway could break the Rights of Way barrier easier. Eighteen months later, Jobs announced iPods. Jobs was right about the barrier. Massachusetts broke it in 2021.
