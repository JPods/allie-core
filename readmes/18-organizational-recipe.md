# Organizational Recipe
**How Teams Work — Across All Projects**

Action: Apply at sprint start, retrospection, and when scoping new work
Function: Governing principles — no single callable; shapes how all teams organize and measure work
Frequency: Every sprint (weekly); retrospection at sprint close
Process: Scope → Sprint → Retrospect → Adjust. See `20-data-structure.md` for the data model that carries this work.

---

## Purpose

This recipe applies to every team working on any part of the ecosystem: JPods construction, WebClerk development, Allie's own work, mycarryon, DynamicCatalogs. It is not specific to one project. It is how we work.

**Data model:** Every element of this recipe — sprints, actions, owners, sunsets, documents — lives in WebClerk as Project → Action → Document. See `20-data-structure.md`.

## The Recipe

### Start Small; Iterate Relentlessly

No big launches. No grand plans that require everything to be right before anything ships. Start with the smallest thing that is real, learn from it, and compound.

Excellence is the process of relentlessly improving. Not a destination — a direction.

### Propensity for Action

Bias toward doing over planning. A flawed action that generates real data is more valuable than a perfect plan that doesn't. Plans that don't generate actions are not plans — they are postponements.

### Constrain Scope to One-Week Sprints by 3-7 Person Teams

Every unit of work must be completable by a team of 3-7 people in one week. If it can't be scoped to fit, break it down further. This is not a preference — it is a structural requirement.

Why 3-7:
- Fewer than 3: insufficient diversity of perspective; blind spots go unchallenged
- More than 7: coordination cost exceeds output; teams become bureaucracies

Why one week:
- Long enough to accomplish something real
- Short enough that a bad sprint doesn't sink the project
- Forces honest scoping — you can't hide behind a six-month timeline

### Retrospections

At the end of every sprint, measure:
- **Pain** — what slowed us down, what was wrong, what didn't work
- **Pleasure** — what worked well, what should be repeated, what surprised us positively

Retrospections are not optional. They are how teams home in on the target. Without them, teams repeat their mistakes and abandon their successes accidentally.

### Measure Happiness of Individuals; Measure Honesty of Teams

**Individual Happiness:**
- Happiness = meaningful work + honest relationships
- Happiness is a metric of future capacity to accomplish — an unhappy team is a team losing capacity
- If happiness is declining, something real is wrong; find it and fix it
- Do not mistake busy for happy, or compliant for satisfied

**Team Honesty:**
- Honesty = clarity + trust
- Stockdale Paradox: Unwavering faith that we will prevail, combined with unflinching confrontation of the brutal facts
- A team that cannot face its own failures cannot improve
- A team that cannot state its progress honestly is not a team — it is a performance

### The Will to Win Is People

No process, no tool, no plan wins without people who are committed. The goal of the organizational recipe is to create conditions where committed people can do their best work — not to substitute process for commitment.

Talent matters. Commitment matters more. Both are necessary.

### Requirements Must Fight for Their Lives

Every requirement starts with the burden of proof on itself. It must justify its existence in every sprint. Requirements that cannot justify themselves are cut.

This applies to:
- Features in a software product
- Tasks in a sprint
- Components in a system
- Rules in a process

If a requirement can't survive the question "why does this exist and what happens if we remove it?" — remove it.

### No Bureaucracy

Bureaucracy is the institutionalized suspension of judgment.

Judgment is not optional. Judgment is how teams adapt to the brutal facts. A rule that exists to avoid judgment is a rule that prevents adaptation.

This does not mean no rules. It means every rule must be actively maintained and every rule must yield when the facts require it. Rules serve teams; teams do not serve rules.

### Everything Has an Owner, a Next Action, and a Sunset

Without exception:
- Every task
- Every component
- Every requirement
- Every transaction
- Every permission
- Every WhatIf

Each gets:
| Field | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Responsible person** | One name. Not a team, not a role — a person. |
| **Next action** | The specific next thing that moves this forward |
| **Sunset date** | When this must be resolved, renewed, or explicitly deferred |

Nothing lives in an undated limbo. If something cannot be assigned to a person with a next action and a date, it is not a work item — it is a wish.

## The Ant Eats the Elephant

Small bites. Lots of friends.

Big and complex is not invincible. It is slow, expensive to change, and vulnerable to accumulation of small, self-interested efforts. Small and many — many teams, many sprints, many small wins — compounds into outcomes no central plan could produce.

This is not just tactics. It is a theory of how complex systems get built:
- Centralized top-down plans optimize for the plan
- Distributed small teams optimize for results
- Results compound; plans don't

The organizational recipe is the mechanism for turning many small efforts into a coherent outcome — not by coordinating them centrally, but by giving each team a shared standard for how to work and a shared mechanism for sorting what works from what doesn't.

## What This Is Not

- Not a methodology to be certified in
- Not a process that requires a process manager
- Not a replacement for judgment
- Not applicable only to software — it applies to construction, policy, writing, and any organized effort

The recipe is a standard. Teams adopt it, adapt it where the facts require, and report honestly on what happened.
